ISSUE 3 12/1/2004

 

Revised Business Plan for 2003/4 year

Introduction

This business plan updates and supersedes the one presented and accepted at the July 2003 board meeting. This revised plan reflects the trading conditions in the second half of 2003 calendar year, and the intentions of the company to acquire further companies during 2004. As these negotiations are still ongoing there has to be some degree of variance in the actual dates when the new companies will be absorbed into the existing operations. The Board at their meeting on Thursday 8th January 2004 adopted the revised plan.

Background.

Company intends to continue to concentrate on the technical rather than commodity end of injection moulding, in order to maintain the higher margins available on the technical work. However there are now severe pressures on margins on the technical moulding work as moulding companies all scramble to win this work. The existing operation has been restructured and organised to service a much higher volume of business than it is currently processing and can therefore absorb a considerable expansion of turnover, without making significant changes to the overhead costs. Over the past year the Company has seen a 120% increase in turnover, of which 77% has been organic growth. The rest has come from an acquisition made in July 2002. The first 4 months of the current financial year have already secured a further 27% organic growth and this is expected to continue to grow over the remaining portion of the year. However, it is believed that more dramatic growth still can be achieved by the acquisition of other companies and this should boost the annualised turnover of the company to around the £3m mark by the financial year-end. The Company has demonstrated that it can manage its growth by returning a small profit on 8 of the last 10 months of trading.

Over the last year, the management team has been working together and planning for the next stage of their expansion. The team comprises the core management group encompassing the founder and Managing Director, with a very strong technical moulding background; the Sales Manager with very good commercial awareness; the acting General Manager / Chairman with very good industrial background; the Quality Manager with very strong Automotive / Aerospace systems background; the Purchasing Manager with a very strong plastics background; the acting Production Manager with very strong shop floor technical skills and the Office Manager who ensures that all the commercial and system matters are kept in line. Behind all these key team members are their departments, dedicated to meeting the highest possible customer satisfaction objectives, which in turn will help drive the whole company forward.

After detailed analysis work 3 companies have been selected and have declared that they are interested in becoming part of the Company, subject to contract exchange. These companies have been selected because they are also technical moulders and have complimentary work and processes to that already undertaken by the Company. The owners of the companies are all seeking a retirement exit route and their aspirations can be met through a commission based payment on the sales transferred to the Company. There will be minimal up-front payments to these companies, but there will be the incorporation, legal and physical costs of the move to be funded as well as the site closure costs. Where possible cost offsets will be done with the former owners of these businesses to minimise the cash requirements of the acquisitions.

Outline information on the 3 target companies.

At this stage the names of the 3 companies are not being disclosed for reasons of confidentiality and non-disclosure undertakings given to their owners, so they are referred to as company 1,2 & 3. Some general information is given about each company and target completion dates.

Company 1 is based in the North Midlands and has a current turnover of just over £0.5m. They manufacture technical mouldings on 25-270t machines, a small amount of presswork and have their own small tool room. They supply a number of relatively small businesses around their general locality but their physical location is not thought to be fundamental to their business. Component, tool design and manufacture are also offered. The company is operating out of fairly run down premises and the owners are looking to retire and emigrate once the business is transferred and bedded down. The company is making a profit of around £40k at present and this should be increased, after commissions, to around £140k after transfer into the Company, by saving a major part of their existing overhead structure, as these services and costs are already in place at the Company. This would be the last business to transfer and should be starting to move by the end of July 2004.

Company 2 is based in the South and has a current turnover of £0.8m. They manufacture small technical mouldings mainly on 15-100t machines and have their own rudimentary tool repair room. They supply a very large number of customers with a combination of trade mouldings and own products. The company is operating out of a variety of fairly run down non-contiguous premises and the owner is looking to retire once the business is transferred and bedded down. The company is making a small loss of around £2k at present and this would be changed into a profit, after commissions, of around £150k post transfer into the Company, by saving a major part of their existing overhead structure, as these services and costs are already in place at the Company. We plan to make this the first business to transfer and this should be starting to move by the end of March 2004.

Company 3 is based in the Midlands and has a current turnover of £0.6m. They manufacture technical mouldings and a small amount of commodity mouldings on 23-300t machines and have no tool room. They supply mouldings to a number of automotive and industrial customers. The company is operating out of fairly run down premises and the owners are looking to exit from the business once transferred and bedded down. The company is making a profit of around £35k at present and this should be increased, after commissions, to around £235k after transfer into the Company, by saving a major part of their existing overhead structure, as these services and costs are already in place at the Company. This should be the second business to transfer and this should be starting to move by the end of May 2004.

Integration of the 3 companies into the Company.

As indicated above it is planned to integrate the companies into the Company operation at 2 monthly intervals. This should allow time to bed down the new work and assimilate the customer base. It will require a lot of office administrative and technical work over this period to ensure a smooth transition. It will also probably require the previous owners to spend at least a month at the Company during each initial 2 month period after the transfer from their site. Economically we will need to close down their operation as fast as possible in order to crystalise the savings in overheads. It is planned that their sites should be completely closed no later than 2 months from the start of their move to the Company site.

In general it is believed that the underlying structure of the existing Company site and operational systems will be able to expand to incorporate the extra workload. However there are some key areas which will need to be addressed and will require some expenditure.

Staffing. The extra workload will require us to re-instate the 3rd shift that was laid off in the autumn of 2003. This will require the recruitment of an additional setter / supervisor and the necessary operators to run the shift. This is already in the Company budget. It is also believed that the site will need a proper site production manager. This role is at present split across a number of functions. It is not believed that there is a candidate on site capable to undertaking the function so a new person will have to be recruited. It is also believed that it will be necessary to recruit a couple more apprentices for the manufacturing operation. One will be needed to help out on day-to-day tasks on the Moulding shop floor and a second one to support the QC / inspection function. When company 3 comes onto the site (May 2004) it will be necessary to demonstrate that the Company is running to the TS automotive quality standards. From initial evaluations undertaken at the Company it is believed that the site is already almost running to this standard so there should not be too much extra work to implement and be certified to this standard. The above extra staff will cost about £50k with NI but it is believed that equivalent savings (from their direct operating costs) should be achievable from the incoming 3 companies, in addition to those savings already shown from their overheads.

Systems. The extra volume of work will show up some of the shortcomings in the present manually operated MRP system. A proper MRP systems suite will have to be installed on site and this will need suitable familiarisation and training time. The system should ideally be installed ahead of the transfer of the first company in order to avoid double handling of their data. In addition to the software cost of between £10-20k there will be a further spend of about £10k on hardware.

Space. It has been planned for a while to install a Mezzanine floor over the toolroom and some of the offices at the Company. It has not been done already, as the volume of work had not justified it yet. With the arrival of the extra workload it will have to be installed in order to create more space on the shop floor. It will have the added advantage of removing most of the operators off the shop floor, which will in turn allow some of the moulding machines to be run closer together, generating space for additional machines, as and when needed. In order to get the mouldings away from the moulding machines and up to the Mezzanine floor a series of elevating conveyors will be needed. In due course robots on the larger machines will feed these. To start with a few shorter feed conveyors can be used from the larger moulding machines. The mezzanine and the conveyors will require about £50k of investment.

Other infrastructure items. The workload for company 2 includes a lot of work that is handled by about 20 home workers. In due course it is expected that their moulds will be adapted / modified to reduce, but will not fully eliminate, the amount of home working that is required. There are not enough local people in wanting to undertake the home working so it will be necessary to go slightly further a field. Almost certainly this will mean that we will have to arrange the delivery and collection of the work so a small van will become necessary. The van would be leased. The availability of such a vehicle will also have other uses, which will save having to use the current Sales Manager or MD’s vehicle for a variety of collection and delivery trips.

There will be other infrastructure items that will be required, such as storage for more tools, drawings, papers and records. All of this should be available for only a nominal cost from the closure of the 3 acquired companies. Likewise if it is decided that any of the main capital items of plant are required from the acquired companies, either because of capacity or the fact that their plant offers advantages over the existing Company plant, then the aim will be to acquire it at nominal cost or on favourable lease terms from the acquired company.

Closure assumptions for the 3 companies.

In this business plan it has been assumed that the 3 acquired companies will all be acquired on the following basis.

1. The owners will relinquish control of their companies in return for a percentage fee based on the actual sales made by the Company on the acquired turnover for a set number of years. This fee will be paid on a quarterly basis in arrears. This arrangement offers the former owners a tax efficient retirement route and an ongoing income stream for a number of years.

2. The owners will be responsible for the sale and disposal of all remaining plant and equipment on their site, termination of the lease and any staff closure costs. Anything left over from the sale, after payment of closure costs, to be theirs. In effect the Company will therefore be acquiring mainly the order book and tools from the companies. The Company may choose to select some plant and sundry equipment for purchase and transfer to the Company site at auction prices or on rental terms to be agreed with the former owners.

3. The Company will be responsible for the transfer of all stock, whether of raw materials or finished goods, and the transfer of all tools and fixtures. Details of any payments for raw materials and stock will be worked out on a case-by-case basis. Transfer of title of any wholly owned tools or fixtures would pass to the Company as part of the contract, as will the company names once the operation is closed.

4. The former owners may be given limited rights of access / seat on the Company Board for a specified period after their acquisition.

Investment sought and shareholding implications.

In order to strengthen the company finances, provide funds for the costs of the acquisitions and some infrastructure modifications an investment of £250,000 is being sought from an external investor. Ideally this would be as a lump sum but could be phased, to some degree, as shown in the cash flow forecast. In order to attract the investor quickly, it is believed that we will need to allocate up to 25% of the shareholding in the company to this investor. The existing 3 shareholders would therefore have to drop their existing shareholding proportionately. Depending on the new shareholders wishes there may be a Board seat option. The new shareholder should therefore see a return of their investment via dividends in about 2 years and the potential for a 2.5 times return in 5 years. In the longer term there may be the possibility of an AIM float if the company continues its growth at the same rate in future years.

The timing of the growth is now critical as the target companies have been identified and have declared they are ready to be acquired. If we delay the acquisitions then there is a danger that someone else may acquire them, and a delay would have further implications on the cash requirements for the ongoing Company operations. It is accepted that the growth plan is ambitious but the benefits flowing from the growth are such that it is believed that it is the best route forward for the Company.

The Company offers the following services.

Design

2 Dimensional Design:

AutoCAD LT® Allows us to Design and Present information quickly on a computer; and make any necessary changes with greater ease. We also have the luxury of delivering you drawings instantly across the Internet via e-mail!

3 Dimensional Design:

We use a design package called Solidworks 2000®. With a highly trained design team, we can create almost anything in 3 dimensions. This can help us see what the final part will look like, and modify the design at an early stage.

Moldflow analysis:

With an excellent new package, called Moldflow, we can predict the way a part is going to fill when we commence production. With this package, we can select the optimum injection point(s), the best machine to use and also, how much time will be needed to produce each part; thus giving us the opportunity to provide accurate and  detailed production information about the part we have developed.

To further enhance the vision of the design, we can produce rapid prototypes using the latest computer technology. From this, solid models can be manufactured quickly and cost-effectively, using the STL file format; generating models in SLA and SLS. These models can also be used to provide small quantities of components, using vacuum casting.

CNC Manufacture:

We use two programs together to give us the capability of taking a 3-Dimensional model, or a 2-Dimensional engineering drawing, and make a CNC program to    produce a high quality tool that fits the specifications of the model exactly. We use Alpha Cam and Alpha Edit. Alpha Cam is the design part of this process, and Alpha Edit manipulates the drawing that we produce into a language that the CNC machinery can understand.

Internet and Communication:

We have the capability to send animated, detailed designs over the Internet, containing our notes ideas and modifications. We can also give details of our findings during the design stage in an informative and professional layout, simply by e-mail.

Prototyping:

All the latest rapid prototyping and tooling methods are available to us, allowing final adjustments and perfection of the design, still at a very low cost.

Toolmaking

 Our in-house capabilities cover most of the processes required to manufacture even the most complicated mould tools. Our CNC machines are linked directly to our design office via DNC, allowing seamless integration of the two services and pinpoint accuracy. Spark erosion machines ranging from ten to fifty amps. For accuracy, all tool room machinery is equipped with D.R.O technology. We also house a collection of manually operated machines, such as lathes, milling machines, grinding machines and polishing equipment, operated by a highly skilled and dedicated engineering team.

Injection Moulding

 

We can cater for many different sizes of components, ranging from 0.2 of a gram, up to 1.5KG, producing from 1 to 1,000,000 plus. The moulding machines in-house range from 5 up to 480 tons, allowing us to mould a great many materials, and to accept varying mould sizes and designs.

We are currently holding machines produced by; Arburg, Dr. BOY, DEMAG, mallpower, Negri Bossi and Austin Allen. These machines are all complimented by various mould temperature control systems using liquids and induction heating, allowing us to make full use of a wide range of materials. In critical applications, the mould temperature is continually monitored by an onboard CPU, supplying total control over the moulding process. We carry a full compliment of material drying and dehumidifying equipment enabling the processing of hygroscopic plastics on site.

The thermoplastics that we regularly use include:

Polypropylenes polyethylenes Nylons, Elastomers thermoplastic rubbers, polyurethanes, polyesters through to engineering and speciality materials such as Polybutylterapthalate (PBT), acetal, polycarbonates, polyphenol sulphides (PPS), liquid crystal polymers (LCP) and some fluoropolymers.

Of course, if you need any specific material needs, we can provide advice and the service required to realise your designs.

 We welcome technically demanding and precise projects, including insert and outsert moulding, asymmetrical material 2 shot mouldings and encapsulations, smaller production runs, as well as less specialised projects.
